When a steady hand costs you millions.

A seventeen-year look at how the City of Troy, Michigan pension fund performed against the broad U.S. stock market.

Source data · Annual Comprehensive Financial Reports of the City of Troy

Troy's pension fund is among the best-funded municipal plans in Michigan, with a fiduciary net position covering more than 150% of obligations as of fiscal year 2025. It earned that distinction not by chasing returns, but by holding a diversified mix of stocks, bonds, real estate, and private equity. The cost of that prudence shows up clearly when you set its track record next to a 100% S&P 500 portfolio.

§ 04Reading the numbers

The S&P 500 is the wrong yardstick for a public pension. It's used here because it's the universally recognized benchmark, not because it would be a prudent allocation for a fiduciary obligation.

A public pension fund's job is to make a fixed stream of benefit payments to retirees, on schedule, for decades. The Board of Trustees uses an assumed discount rate of 6.50% in Troy's case — meaning the plan is required to generate at least that much, on average, to remain fully funded. Troy's 7.03% annualized return clears that bar.

An all-equity portfolio would have generated higher returns, but at the cost of multi-year drawdowns that could force the city to make emergency cash contributions during recessions. The point of diversification is visible in the FY2008 and FY2009 columns above: while the S&P 500 fell by a combined 35%, Troy's diversified portfolio fell about 17%. The plan kept paying benefits without the city writing a panic check.

What the plan actually holds

As of FY2025, roughly 60% in U.S. and international equities, 26% in fixed income, plus smaller allocations to private equity, real estate, and cash. The Board of Trustees selects external investment managers; MissionSquare administers the related defined contribution accounts.

Why the plan is so well funded

The defined benefit plan is closed to new entrants. Liabilities are shrinking as retirees draw benefits, while the asset pool continues to compound. Troy has not been required to make an employer pension contribution in seven of the last ten years.

A fairer benchmark

A 60/40 portfolio (60% stocks, 40% bonds) returned roughly 7.5–8% annualized over this same window — far closer to Troy's 7.03% than the S&P 500's 9.23%. The pension fund performed roughly in line with comparable diversified portfolios.

Who runs it

The Employees Retirement System Board of Trustees — eight members including the City Manager and CFO, with appointees from City Council and the membership. The Board meets the second Wednesday of each month at Troy City Hall. Meetings are open to the public.

METHODOLOGY. Troy returns for FY2014–FY2025 are the annual money-weighted rates of return, net of investment expenses, as reported in the Schedule of Investment Returns under GASB Statement 67. For FY2008–FY2013 (predating GASB 67), returns are computed as Total Investment Earnings divided by Beginning Total Net Position of the Employees' Retirement System trust. For FY2008–FY2010 this trust held both pension and retiree healthcare assets in a single pool; for FY2012 onward it is pension-only. The FY2014 simple-method calculation yields 21.84% versus the GASB-67 official 22.23%, confirming the two methods are closely comparable.
