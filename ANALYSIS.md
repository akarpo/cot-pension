# City of Troy Pension Analysis

Independent analysis of the **City of Troy, Michigan Employees Retirement System** investment performance from fiscal year 2008 through fiscal year 2025, benchmarked against the S&P 500 Total Return Index.

---

## Headline findings

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Troy pension annualized return (FY08–FY25) | **7.03%** |
| S&P 500 Total Return annualized (FY08–FY25) | **9.23%** |
| Annual performance gap | **−2.20 pp** |
| Plan funded ratio, FY2025 | **152.03%** (best-funded municipal plan in MI) |
| Net pension asset, FY2025 | **$81.0M** |
| Pension trust assets, FY2025 | **$236.7M** |
| Growth of $1M in Troy pension (FY08–FY25) | **$3.18M** |
| Growth of $1M in S&P 500 over same period | **$4.49M** |

The pension trailed the S&P 500 in every year from FY2010 through FY2025 — **fifteen consecutive years of underperformance averaging roughly 4 percentage points annually**. The two years of outperformance (FY2008–FY2009) came during the financial crisis, when the bond and fixed-income allocation cushioned the equity drawdown. But that cushion has been repaid many times over by the fifteen-year lag that followed. For a plan that is 152% funded, closed to new entrants, and carrying an $81M net pension asset, the conventional defense of a diversified portfolio — *we need bonds to weather a drawdown without forced selling* — does not apply with much force. The opportunity cost is large, real, and accrues primarily to the intermediaries who manage the non-equity sleeves of the portfolio rather than to the retirees and taxpayers the trust exists to serve.

## Who manages the fund

The pension is governed by the **City of Troy Employees Retirement System Board of Trustees**, established under Chapter 10 of the Troy City Code.

- **Board composition**: 8 members — City Manager, Assistant City Manager/Finance, three appointees of City Council, three appointees of the membership, plus one non-voting retiree representative.
- **City liaison**: Financial Services Director, 248.524.3330.
- **Meetings**: Second Wednesday of each month, 12:00 PM, Troy City Hall Council Board Room. Open to the public.
- **Defined contribution administrator** (separate from the DB plan): MissionSquare (formerly ICMA-RC), with Doug Featherstone as point of contact.

The Board selects external investment managers; those manager names are not published on the city's website but would appear in the ACFR's Investment Section if needed.

## What's in the plan

As of the FY2025 ACFR (Statement of Fiduciary Net Position, June 30, 2025):

| Asset class | Approximate share |
|---|---|
| Common stocks and equity mutual funds | ~60% |
| Corporate bonds and fixed income | ~26% |
| Private equity partnerships | ~5% |
| Real estate | ~2% |
| Cash and U.S. government securities | balance |

The defined benefit plan is **closed to new entrants**. Liabilities are shrinking as retirees draw benefits; the asset pool continues to compound. The city has not made an employer pension contribution in seven of the last ten years because the plan is so well-funded.

The Board uses an **assumed discount rate of 6.50%** to measure plan liabilities. Troy's actual 7.03% annualized return over the FY08–FY25 window clears that bar.

## Reading the numbers

### Why the S&P 500 is a defensible yardstick here

A 100% equity benchmark is often dismissed as inappropriate for a pension fund on the grounds that multi-year drawdowns could force benefit cuts or emergency contributions. That argument applies to underfunded plans operating close to their liability coverage. **It does not describe the City of Troy pension.**

As of FY2025, the plan is **152% funded**, holds a **net pension asset of $81M**, and is **closed to new entrants** with a shrinking liability profile. The city has skipped its employer contribution in seven of the last ten years because the plan is so overcapitalized. A fund in this position has *more* risk capacity than a struggling plan, not less — it could absorb a serious equity drawdown without impairing its ability to pay scheduled benefits. The standard fiduciary-prudence defense of a heavy bond allocation is calibrated for a plan that does not look like this one.

### The cost in dollars

The 2.20 pp annual gap compounds aggressively. Each $1M invested at the start of FY2008:

- Grew to **$3.18M** under the actual Troy allocation
- Would have grown to **$4.49M** in the S&P 500 TR

That is a **$1.31M shortfall per million invested — roughly 29% less terminal wealth**. Scaled against the trust's asset base, the foregone investment earnings over this period plausibly run into the **tens of millions of dollars** — capital that would otherwise belong to retirees or, on the other side of the ledger, would have permitted larger surplus distributions or contribution holidays for taxpayers.

### The 60/40 defense doesn't survive contact with the funded ratio

A standard 60/40 portfolio returned roughly **7.5–8% annualized** over this window, close to Troy's 7.03%. The conventional reading is that Troy performed in line with a sensible peer benchmark.

But this comparison **takes the asset allocation as the thing to be defended**. The relevant question is whether a 60/40-style allocation was appropriate for a plan with this much excess capital and this little need for volatility dampening. Holding ~40% of an overfunded, closed-plan portfolio in fixed income — much of it municipal and investment-grade — locked in low-single-digit yields during a 17-year window when equities returned ~9%. At a moment when the plan had no fiduciary need to suppress volatility, the bond sleeve was a luxury, not a necessity. The yields earned on those bonds came nowhere close to compensating for the equity returns forgone.

### The bond and alternatives sleeves benefit fund managers more than beneficiaries

A passive S&P 500 index fund costs **3–5 basis points** all-in. A multi-asset book with active fixed income, private equity partnerships, real estate, and investment consultants typically runs an order of magnitude higher — **50–100+ basis points** when manager fees, consultant retainers, custody, and (for private equity) carried interest are aggregated honestly.

The 5% private equity allocation and 2% real estate allocation are the most expensive sleeves on a per-dollar basis. Private equity "2 and 20" fee structures, real estate management fees, and active fixed-income management all transfer a meaningful share of gross returns to general partners, advisors, and intermediaries before any return reaches the trust. For a plan that is already 152% funded and does not need higher expected returns, **these allocations primarily generate fees for managers and consultants rather than risk-adjusted return for the trust**.

The municipal bond holdings specifically deserve scrutiny on these grounds: their yields are tax-advantaged for individual investors but offer no tax benefit to a tax-exempt pension trust. To justify owning them inside a pension, the yield would need to clear a much higher hurdle than it does — high enough to offset the opportunity cost of not being in equities. It does not.

### Disclosed management fees, FY2015–FY2025

Beginning with fiscal year 2015, the City of Troy ACFR breaks investment expense out as a discrete line on the *Statement of Changes in Fiduciary Net Position* for the Employees' Retirement System. Eleven years of disclosure are now on the public record:

| Fiscal Year | Pension trust investment expense |
|---:|---:|
| FY2015 | $315,083 |
| FY2016 | $415,732 |
| FY2017 | $341,260 |
| FY2018 | $461,309 |
| FY2019 | $462,681 |
| FY2020 | $288,316 |
| FY2021 | $500,458 |
| FY2022 | $473,172 |
| FY2023 | $549,727 |
| FY2024 | $609,069 |
| FY2025 | $620,775 |
| **11-year total** | **$5,037,582** |

Over this eleven-year window the trust paid approximately **$5.0M in directly invoiced investment management expense** — averaging ~$458K per year and rising as assets grew from ~$190M to ~$237M. The implied effective fee rate sits around **20–28 basis points** of beginning assets across the period.

For FY2008–FY2014 the ACFRs do not break investment expense out as a separate line; it is netted within "Net adjustment in fair value of investments." Applying the observed ~20 bps rate to those years' beginning pension trust assets yields an estimated additional **$1.5–1.8M** of management expense across the seven-year pre-disclosure period — bringing a conservative 17-year cumulative total to roughly **$6.5–7M of directly invoiced fees**.

### Why that $5–7M number is a floor, not a ceiling

The disclosed "investment expense" line captures fees that are **invoiced directly to the trust** — typically the investment consultant's retainer and a portion of separate-account manager fees. It does **not** capture:

- **Embedded mutual fund expense ratios.** Fees on commingled and pooled equity / fixed-income funds are netted into reported NAV and never surface as a line item in the ACFR.
- **Private equity management fees and carried interest.** Troy's ~5% private equity sleeve operates under "2 and 20"-style economics — typically a 1.5–2% annual management fee plus 20% carry on profits — all of which is taken out at the fund level before any return reaches the trust.
- **Real estate fund-level fees.** The ~2% real estate allocation pays property and asset management fees inside the fund structures.
- **Custody, audit, and certain consultant retainers** that are sometimes classified under "Administrative expenses" rather than investment expense.

A reasonable all-in fee estimate for Troy's actual mix — roughly 60% equities, 26% fixed income, 5% private equity, 2% real estate, balance cash — is in the range of **50–80 basis points** annually. Applied to the trust's average asset base of ~$200M, the all-in fee load over the full 17 years is plausibly in the **$17–30M** range, of which only ~$5–7M is visible on the ACFRs' face. The remainder is paid out of gross investment returns before the net number is reported, and is therefore invisible in the published statements.

### Putting fees alongside the opportunity cost

The two numbers measure different things and stack:

| | 17-year cumulative (approx.) |
|---|---:|
| **Opportunity cost vs. S&P 500 TR** (foregone investment earnings on the trust's average asset base) | **tens of millions of dollars** |
| **Directly invoiced management fees** (ACFR disclosure, plus pre-2015 estimate) | **$5–7M** |
| **All-in management fees including embedded PE/RE/MF fees** (estimated) | **$17–30M** |

The opportunity cost is what the beneficiaries and taxpayers gave up by holding a diversified mix in an overfunded closed plan; the fee total is what they paid to the intermediaries who manage that diversification. Both move the same direction and reinforce the same conclusion: the current allocation transfers wealth from the trust to the firms that manage its non-equity sleeves, and the conventional fiduciary-prudence defense does not justify that transfer for a plan with the City of Troy's funded ratio.

### Where the 2008–2009 outperformance went

The defense of the current allocation often points to FY2008 and FY2009, when Troy's diversified mix beat a 100% equity benchmark by 13–18 percentage points. That outperformance was real — and was paid back with interest. The two crisis years bought roughly 21 pp of cumulative relative outperformance; the following fifteen years gave back roughly 60 pp of cumulative relative underperformance. **The net effect of the diversification across the full 17-year window is sharply negative.** The bond-cushion story is a story about two years, not seventeen.

### What this analysis is

This is an argument that the City of Troy pension has **materially underperformed a reasonable benchmark over 17 years**; that the gap has compounded to tens of millions of dollars in foregone wealth; that the conventional fiduciary-prudence defense of bond and alternatives allocations does not apply with much force to an overfunded, closed plan; and that the practical beneficiaries of the current allocation are the intermediaries who manage the non-equity sleeves rather than the retirees and taxpayers the trust exists to serve.

Nothing here asserts that the Board has breached its fiduciary duty. The argument is narrower and prospective: at the margin, with the plan in its current overfunded and closed state, more equity exposure would likely have produced — and going forward, would likely produce — substantially more wealth for the trust, with risk that the plan's funded ratio is more than equipped to absorb.

## Methodology

### Troy pension returns

- **FY2014–FY2025**: Official money-weighted rate of return, net of investment expenses, as reported in the *Schedule of Investment Returns* in each year's ACFR. This disclosure is required under GASB Statement 67, which took effect for fiscal years beginning after June 15, 2013.
- **FY2008–FY2013**: Pre-GASB-67. Returns are computed as `Total Investment Earnings ÷ Beginning Total Net Position` of the Employees' Retirement System trust, extracted from the Statement of Changes in Fund Net Assets in each ACFR.
  - FY2008–FY2010: ERS trust held both pension and retiree healthcare (OPEB) assets in one investment pool. Returns reflect the combined trust.
  - FY2012–FY2013: OPEB was split off into its own trust starting FY2012. Returns are pension-only from FY2012 forward.

### Cross-validation

For FY2014 — the only year where both methods can be computed — the simple-return method yields **21.84%** while the official GASB-67 money-weighted return is **22.23%**. The 0.39 pp gap confirms the two methods produce comparable results, and the pre-GASB-67 figures in this analysis are reliable as approximations.

### Why FY2011 is omitted

Two reasons stack:
1. The City's published FY2011 ACFR is **image-scanned** (not OCR'd) — the financial statement pages are pictures, so the numbers cannot be extracted programmatically with confidence.
2. The pension trust composition **changed mid-period** as the Retiree Healthcare Trust was separated from the main ERS. The beginning-of-year and end-of-year balances are not directly comparable.

Including FY2011 with poor-quality data would damage the integrity of the time series. Excluding it leaves a clearly disclosed gap.

### S&P 500 returns

Sourced from the **`^SP500TR`** index (S&P 500 Total Return — price + reinvested dividends) via Yahoo Finance. Each fiscal-year return is computed from the index's closing value on the last trading day of June in year X−1 to the last trading day of June in year X, exactly matching Troy's fiscal-year cycle (July 1 – June 30).

### Growth-of-$1M calculations

Starting value: $1,000,000 invested on July 1, 2007 (start of FY2008). At the end of each fiscal year, the value is multiplied by `(1 + annual_return)`. The FY2011 gap in the series is bridged by skipping that year — both Troy and S&P series skip it, so the relative comparison remains valid for years where both data points exist.

Troy publishes its ACFR approximately four months after fiscal year end (June 30). The GFOA's *Certificate of Achievement for Excellence in Financial Reporting* program — which Troy has won for 27 consecutive years — requires submission within 180 days of fiscal year end.

## Reproducing the analysis

```bash
# Install dependencies
pip install yfinance pandas

# Run the dataset builder (re-pulls S&P 500 from Yahoo Finance)
python scripts/build_dataset.py
```

The script writes a fresh `pension_data.json` and prints the cumulative growth and annualized return calculations.

To rebuild from PDFs (e.g. to verify the pre-GASB-67 numbers):

```bash
# Extract the pension trust statements from each ACFR
pdftotext -layout source-data/Troy_ACFR_FY2008.pdf - | grep -A 20 "Total investment"
pdftotext -layout source-data/Troy_ACFR_FY2008.pdf - | grep -A 5 "beginning of year"
```

The hardcoded values in `build_dataset.py` (e.g. `fy2008_inv = -8_820_252`) come directly from those statements.

## Deploying the webpage to GitHub Pages

1. Create a new public repo on GitHub named `cot-pension`.
2. From this directory, run:
   ```bash
   cd cot-pension
   git init
   git add .
   git commit -m "Initial commit: Troy pension analysis FY2008-FY2025"
   git branch -M main
   git remote add origin https://github.com/<your-username>/cot-pension.git
   git push -u origin main
   ```
3. On github.com, go to **Settings → Pages**.
4. Under "Build and deployment": Source = `Deploy from a branch`, branch = `main`, folder = `/webpage` (since the site is in the `webpage/` subfolder).
5. Save. The page will be live at `https://<your-username>.github.io/cot-pension/` within a minute.

If GitHub Pages requires the webpage at the repo root, just move `index.html` and `data.js` out of `webpage/` and up into the root before pushing.
